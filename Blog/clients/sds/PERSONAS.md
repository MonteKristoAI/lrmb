# SDS / Warrior — Buyer Personas for Blog Content

**Source:** SDS 2026 Brand Voice Manifesto v4, Field Messaging Guide, and Christopher Knight's feedback (2026-04-01, 2026-04-07)

**Purpose:** Every blog post is written for ONE primary persona and MAY serve 1-2 secondaries. The persona defines voice, vocabulary, pain point, and call-to-action. Do not write "for the wireline industry." Write for a specific person sitting at a specific location doing a specific job.

---

## How to Use This File

Before writing any SDS post, open this file and pick a **primary persona**. The post brief in `briefs/XX-slug.md` must specify:
- Primary persona
- Their emotional job (not just functional)
- Their pain point
- Their current workaround
- Their decision criteria
- Where they discovered the topic
- What they will DO after reading

## Persona 1 — Field Service Manager (FSM)

**Also known as:** Perforating FSM, Wireline Ops Manager, Operations Superintendent
**Sits at:** Service company HQ or regional operations office; sometimes at wellsite
**Responsibility:** Running a fleet of wireline trucks across multiple locations; keeping every crew safe, productive, and profitable
**Reports to:** Service Company Leader / VP Operations
**Reports down to:** Crew chiefs, Electronics Technicians, Tool Techs

### Functional job
- Assign jobs to trucks
- Ensure every truck is rigged and running
- Handle field emergencies and unplanned downtime
- Train new crews
- Manage the relationship with operator company men
- Track equipment costs and job economics

### Emotional job
**"Protect the reputation of my company with every operator we serve."** When a wireline job goes wrong, the operator blames the FSM's company. The FSM carries that reputational weight on every crew, every truck, every call.

### Pain points
1. **Fragmentation.** Different gun systems, different software, different panels across the fleet — every new tool is a new failure point.
2. **Crew turnover.** New hires can't reliably run legacy panels; training curve is long.
3. **Getting blamed for things the tool did.** Hard to prove the stage was well-executed when the data record is partial.
4. **Standing by while frac waits.** Wireline failures cost money per minute.
5. **The 2 a.m. phone call.** Something broke at the wellsite and the FSM is the first call.

### Current workarounds
- Runs a mix of panels across trucks; accepts training cost and inefficiency
- Keeps a senior ET on call at all hours
- Relies on "experienced hands" rather than standardized processes

### Decision criteria (when evaluating Warrior)
- Does it reduce crew training time?
- Does it make job outcomes more consistent?
- Does the FSM's phone ring less?
- Can the FSM prove to the operator that the job was executed correctly?

### Language and vocabulary
- "On location", "at the wellsite", "rig up", "run the job"
- "Company man" (operator rep)
- "Get the reel moving", "tension on", "run it in the hole"
- Avoids corporate speak; speaks in operational specifics
- Will say "my guys", "my crew", "my hands"

### Where they read
- Rigzone
- JPT (if SPE member)
- Hart Energy
- LinkedIn groups (wireline, oilfield ops)
- Trade show circuits (OTC, ATCE, NAPE)
- Phone calls with peers at competing service cos

### What they will DO after reading a good post
- Forward it to the VP Ops as context for a purchase conversation
- Save it for the next "why do we need to switch" conversation
- Share it in a LinkedIn comment under a related post

### Content templates that fit FSMs
- **Case study** (real wellsite, real crew, real outcome)
- **Failure mode analysis** (what went wrong, what fixed it)
- **Comparison table** (two approaches, measured outcomes)
- **Regulatory explainer** (what API RP 67 4th edition means for YOUR trucks)

### Search intent
- "Why did my wireline job fail"
- "BSEE safety alert 2024 wireline"
- "How to reduce wireline rig up time"
- "Wireline operations best practices"
- "Standardized wireline fleet"

### Sample CTA language
- "Request a demo of the Warrior Universal Panel"
- "Download the FSM quick-start guide"
- "Talk to the Houston engineers who built your system"

## Persona 2 — Perforating Engineer ("The Shooter")

**Also known as:** Perf Engineer, Frac Wireline Engineer
**Sits at:** Wireline unit on location; mostly in the Permian, Eagle Ford, Marcellus, Bakken
**Responsibility:** Running stage-by-stage perforating during frac operations

### Functional job
- Assemble and check the perforating gun string
- Run the tool in the hole
- Address switches, fire the guns, bring the string back out
- Keep frac schedule on track — every minute costs money
- Handle the emotional weight of live explosives

### Emotional job
**"Deliver consistent, well-executed stages that keep frac operating on schedule — so I'm the one they call next time."** Shooters live and die by reputation. A bad stage means the operator calls somebody else for the next well.

### Pain points (from Christopher's Tab 2 notes)
1. **Gun system swaps on pad.** Client switches gun system mid-well; crew has to reconfigure panel, remember settings, hope nothing's missed.
2. **Addressable switch fragmentation.** Different switches need different panels. A truck that works on Monday can't run Tuesday's job without a reload.
3. **The "rig-floor lottery"** — was this stage clean? Won't know until the next one runs.
4. **Pressure from frac crew.** Every minute wireline waits, frac waits.

### Current workarounds
- Carry multiple panels in the truck
- Memorize 3+ different interfaces
- Senior shooter walks the junior through every reconfig

### Decision criteria
- Does the panel work with every gun system and switch on the market?
- Does it load a new switch config in minutes, not hours?
- Is the interface the SAME regardless of the tool string?
- Does Auto-Perf mode actually run the firing sequence safely?

### Language
- "Stage", "gun string", "switch", "fire", "misrun"
- "On pad", "during pumping", "between stages"
- "Clean stage" vs "bad stage"
- Talks about gun systems by brand (Dyna, Titan, G&H) — knows each one's quirks

### Where they read
- Hart Energy (addressable switches coverage)
- LinkedIn "perforating" groups
- Manufacturer trifolds (Dyna, Titan, G&H)
- Sat phone conversations with other shooters between stages

### What they will DO
- Forward to their FSM as "here's what we should buy"
- Bring it up at the next crew meeting
- Ask their FSM for a trial

### Content templates
- **How-to guide** ("How to load a new addressable switch config in under 5 minutes")
- **Comparison** (DynaStage vs Titan vs G&H, from a Warrior panel)
- **Failure mode analysis** (what went wrong with the last misrun and why)
- **Regulatory primer** (API RP 67 4th edition for perforating crews)

### Search intent
- "Addressable switch panel"
- "DynaStage vs Titan"
- "Perforating stage automation"
- "Auto perf mode"
- "Perforating gun system switch compatibility"
- "Perforating misrun causes"

### Sample CTA
- "See how the Warrior Universal Panel runs every major switch from one interface"
- "Download the addressable switch compatibility matrix"

## Persona 3 — Field Logging Engineer

**Also known as:** Cased Hole Engineer, Open Hole Engineer, Wireline Engineer
**Sits at:** Wireline unit, mostly on pad; oil-and-gas focused; sometimes cased hole production logging, sometimes open hole evaluation

### Functional job
- Run the wireline cable into the well
- Capture tool telemetry — gamma ray, density, neutron, resistivity, sonic, caliper, CCL, etc.
- Deliver the log to the company man
- Interpret log quality in real time

### Emotional job
**"Deliver logs the operator can actually trust — before the tool even hits the surface."** If the data is shaky, the operator doesn't trust the crew. If the data is clean, the operator books the next job.

### Pain points
1. "Off the reel" concerns (Christopher flagged this — use "when a log is given to the customer" instead)
2. Inconsistent data quality across different tool strings
3. Log interpretation questions from the company man at 3 a.m.

### Current workarounds
- Senior engineer validates every log
- Multiple tool runs if data is suspect

### Decision criteria
- Does the software present clean, interpretable data in real time?
- Does the same interface work for every tool (gamma, density, neutron, resistivity)?
- Is the log ready to export in LAS/DLIS for the operator's petrophysicist?

### Language
- "Log quality", "tool response", "depth calibration", "borehole corrections"
- Knows every major tool by API name
- Uses "I ran the log", "we logged this section", "the tool was flat-lining"

### Where they read
- JPT
- SPE papers
- CWLS journals
- Company-internal training docs

### What they will DO
- Add to reference library
- Share with junior engineer for training
- Cite in a lunch-and-learn

### Content templates
- **Technical deep dive** (how Warrior handles gamma ray correction)
- **Tool compatibility matrix**
- **Data format explainer** (LAS vs DLIS)

### Search intent
- "Gamma ray logging software"
- "LAS file viewer"
- "Wireline log quality control"
- "Cased hole production log"
- "Open hole formation evaluation"

### Sample CTA
- "Get the Warrior LAS/DLIS export tutorial"
- "See Warrior's tool compatibility matrix"

## Persona 4 — Electronics Technician / Technology Manager

**Also known as:** Perforating ET, Tech Manager, Shop ET
**Sits at:** Service company shop bench (Houston, Odessa, Williston, etc.)
**Note from Christopher:** "We don't see ETs at shows anymore — we see Technology Managers who USED to be ETs. Adjust the persona accordingly."

### Functional job
- Bench-test panels and downhole tools before deployment
- Diagnose field failures when equipment returns
- Recommend equipment purchases to the FSM/VP Ops
- Manage shop inventory

### Emotional job
**"Close the gap between what tests good in the shop and what actually performs in the hole."** The shop test is a promise to the field. Broken promises waste money.

### Pain points (from Christopher's Tab 4 notes)
1. **Field failures that test fine on the bench.** Hardest problem in the job.
2. **Fragmentation of equipment from multiple eras and vendors.**
3. **Aging how-to documentation** (Christopher said SDS's current docs are outdated)
4. **"We've been using Warrior for years — sell me the NEW panel, not the platform."** ETs already know the Warrior name.

### Current workarounds
- Keep a running mental database of "field failure modes by tool"
- Run longer bench tests than spec requires
- Cross-reference with other techs in Slack or phone calls

### Decision criteria (when evaluating the Universal Panel specifically)
- Does the panel test match the field outcome more reliably?
- Is there fewer variables between bench and field?
- Can the ET use better diagnostic output from the panel itself?
- Does the documentation and user forum actually help?

### Language
- "Bench test", "field return", "failure mode", "diagnostic"
- "Panel", "tool", "surface unit", "telemetry"
- Technical and precise; avoids marketing language

### Where they read
- Warrior user forum
- SPE technical papers
- LinkedIn technical groups
- Email threads with peers

### What they will DO
- Evaluate technically
- Recommend purchase to management
- Contribute to user forum if valuable

### Content templates
- **Technical deep dive**
- **Troubleshooting guide**
- **Diagnostic walkthrough**
- **User forum highlight posts**

### Search intent
- "Wireline panel diagnostic"
- "Warrior universal panel specifications"
- "Wireline surface system troubleshooting"
- "CCL failure modes"

### Sample CTA
- "Join the Warrior user forum"
- "Download the Universal Panel technical data sheet"
- "Talk to the Houston engineers"

## Persona 5 — Tool Technician

**Sits at:** Service company shop bench
**Responsibility:** Assembles, calibrates, and repairs downhole tools

### Functional job
- Assemble tool strings
- Calibrate tools before deployment
- Repair tools returning from the field

### Emotional job
**"Send every tool out reliable; diagnose every return fast."** The tool tech is the last line between a $2M tool failure and a successful run.

### Pain points
- Ever-expanding tool catalog across manufacturers
- Cryptic field failure reports
- Sparse documentation on new tools

### Decision criteria (relevant to blog content)
- Does the surface system capture enough diagnostic data to help the tool tech understand what failed?
- Does the software provide good tool-specific fault reporting?

### Search intent
- Specific tool names
- Calibration procedures

### Content priority
Tool techs are a secondary audience. Most posts target them as "also useful" rather than primary. The exception is a **tool repair / diagnosis from surface data** post.

## Persona 6 — Operator Company Man

**Also known as:** Company Representative, Company Man, Operator Rep
**Sits at:** Wellsite trailer; contracts the wireline crew from the operator side
**Represents:** Oil & gas operator (ExxonMobil, Chevron, EOG, Pioneer, etc.)

### Functional job
- Approves or rejects every service at the wellsite
- Signs off on completed operations
- Reports up to the operator's drilling or completion engineer

### Emotional job
**"Get the job done safely; trust the data when I sign off; keep the operator out of trouble."**

### Pain points
- Wireline delays that hold up frac operations
- Data he cannot verify in real time
- Contractor finger-pointing when something fails

### Decision criteria
- Does the contractor have good equipment?
- Can the company man see the same data the wireline engineer sees?
- Does the wireline crew show up prepared?

### Content templates for this persona
- **Thought leadership** ("What to demand from your wireline crew")

### Search intent

## Anti-personas (audiences we are NOT writing for)

- **Non-oilfield readers** (bloggers, journalists, students outside petroleum engineering). We optimize for oilfield language that will alienate them — that's the point.
- **Academic theorists.** We leave SPE papers to JPT and write for practitioners.
- **AI bot traffic.** We DO write for AI citation models, but as a secondary effect. Human-first always.
- **Operator C-suite.** Too high-level; we write for the engineers who influence them.
