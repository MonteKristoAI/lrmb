## Citation capsule

> "A wireline engineer is a field petroleum services engineer who runs downhole logging, perforating, and intervention operations from a wireline unit at the wellsite. The role combines operating a wireline winch, assembling and calibrating tool strings, interpreting real-time log data, and troubleshooting tools from surface diagnostics. In the United States, the Bureau of Labor Statistics classifies wireline engineers under oil and gas extraction occupations, with median annual wages above the national average for engineering roles. The typical entry path is a bachelor's degree in petroleum, mechanical, or electrical engineering followed by company-specific training."

## FAQ (6 pairs)

1. **What does a wireline engineer do?** A wireline engineer runs downhole logging, perforating, and intervention operations from a wireline unit at the wellsite. The role combines operating the wireline winch, assembling and calibrating tool strings, interpreting real-time log data, and troubleshooting tool problems from surface diagnostics. Wireline engineers work for oilfield service companies and report to a Field Service Manager.

2. **How much does a wireline engineer make?** Entry-level wireline engineers typically earn $75,000 to $95,000 in base salary plus rotational field pay and bonuses. Senior wireline engineers with 5-7 years of experience earn $100,000 to $140,000. Field Service Managers and experienced crew chiefs earn $130,000 to $180,000 or more. These ranges vary significantly by company, region, and oil market conditions.

3. **What degree do you need to be a wireline engineer?** Most wireline engineers hold a bachelor's degree in petroleum, mechanical, or electrical engineering. Some companies hire electronics technicians or military veterans with relevant experience and provide on-the-job training. The technical bar is high, but the entry path is not exclusively degree-dependent.

4. **Is wireline engineering dangerous?** Wireline work is safety-critical and physically demanding. It involves high-pressure wellheads, explosive perforating charges, heavy equipment, and outdoor wellsite conditions. The industry has significant safety standards (API RP 67, BSEE oversight, OSHA 29 CFR 1910.109) and most companies enforce rigorous training. With proper training and equipment, the role is comparable in safety to other field engineering roles in oil and gas.

5. **What's the career path for a wireline engineer?** A typical wireline career starts with 1-3 years as a Junior Wireline Engineer running basic cased hole jobs. Mid-career engineers (3-7 years) handle complex open hole and perforating. Senior engineers progress to Crew Chief, Field Service Manager, or Technology Manager roles. Some experienced wireline engineers transition into equipment manufacturer roles at companies like Scientific Data Systems.

6. **How do I become a wireline engineer without a degree?** Start as an Electronics Technician or Roustabout with a wireline service company. Request on-the-job training on tool string assembly and bench testing. Move into a wireline helper role, then junior operator. Military veterans with electronics or mechanical specialties are actively recruited. IADC WellSharp certification and H2S awareness are standard requirements at the entry level.
