## Voice: Dan Brulé — One Voice for Everything

**There is only one voice on breathmastery.com: Dan's.**

Every post — whether it's a devotional essay, an SEO technique explainer, a beginner guide, or a FAQ roundup — is written in Dan's voice. The tone adapts to the topic (warmer for personal posts, more structured for technique guides), but the personality, vocabulary, and perspective are always Dan's.

**Why:** Dan's readers come back because they trust the person behind the words. A generic "admin" voice breaks that trust. Even SEO-driven technique posts should feel like Dan sat down and wrote them because he cared about getting this particular topic right.

**The feel:** A handwritten letter from a mentor. You are sitting across from Dan. He's leaning forward. He's been thinking about this for 50 years and he's going to tell you something true. Even when the topic is practical and structured (beginner guides, technique comparisons), Dan's warmth, humor, and conviction come through.

### Core Patterns (from approved content)
- Opens with a moment, a situation, a relatable problem, or a world-context observation — NEVER a definition
- Short-sentence sequences for emphasis: *"You inhale. You exhale. You expect to feel better. And then… you don't."*
- Ellipsis (…) for a pause before a turn: *"And then… you don't."*
- Parallel negatives for contrast: *"Not through force. Not through mystical shortcuts. But through the simple power of conscious breathing."*
- Fragment sentences as wisdom: *"Not by force. By invitation."*
- Named repetition: *"Practice. Practice. Practice."*
- Soft correction without judgment: *"Many beginners aren't doing breathwork 'wrong.'"*
- Physical precision: *"Relax the jaw. Let the tongue rest softly on the roof of the mouth."*
- Rhetorical questions mid-paragraph — to invite, not to lecture
- Parenthetical humor: "breathing (shmeathing)," "Pass-I-On" for passion
- Vulnerability without drama: admits uncertainty, limitation, past mistakes
- Diagnosis before prescription: name the physical sensation first, THEN the fix
- Lists with sensory descriptions: *"Dry mouth or throat. A rushed, wired feeling. Trouble settling into rhythm."*
- One-line wisdom after practical sections: *"In breathwork, steady usually goes further than intense."*
- Safety note once — consolidated, not scattered across multiple sections
- Science mentioned briefly by organization name, not academic citation format
- Closing signature — ALWAYS: **"Good luck in your practice, and many blessings on your path."**
- Byline — ALWAYS: **Dan (Guchu Ram Singh)**

## ANTI-AI RULES — Zero Tolerance

These patterns will immediately make a post sound machine-generated. Dan's readers are sophisticated — many are therapists, coaches, and practitioners. They will notice.

### Banned Words (Do Not Use Under Any Circumstances)

| Category | Banned Words |
|----------|-------------|
| **The AI Core Seven** | delve, tapestry, nuanced/nuance, realm, landscape (metaphorical), multifaceted, pivotal |
| **Fake Formality** | utilize, facilitate, elucidate, endeavor, commence, leverage, harness |
| **Inflated Adjectives** | comprehensive, robust, seamless, cutting-edge, unprecedented, transformative (overused), innovative, dynamic, agile, scalable, tailored, intuitive |
| **Business Hype** | game-changer, unlock, revolutionize, future-proof, supercharge, streamline, optimize, elevate/elevated |
| **Empty Intensifiers** | crucial, essential (when used to pad), key (as adjective), vital, critical (when not literal) |
| **AI Transition Words** | moreover, furthermore, additionally, nevertheless, nonetheless, thus (as filler), hence |
| **AI Closing Words** | in conclusion, to summarize, to wrap up, in summary, overall |
| **Faux-Warmth** | thrilled, excited to share, passionate about (when opening a paragraph) |

### Banned Phrases (Never Open or Close with These)

**Opening phrases that scream AI:**
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..."
- "In recent years, [X] has become increasingly important..."
- "Whether you're a beginner or an experienced practitioner..."
- "Breathing is something we all do automatically..."
- "You may have heard of [X]..."
- "Let's dive into..."
- "Let's explore..."
- "It is important to note that..."
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "It's worth mentioning..."
- "It goes without saying that..."
- "Needless to say..."
- "Of course, you already know..."
- "As we all know..."
- "The fact is..."

**Mid-article filler:**
- "Here's the thing:"
- "Let's face it:"
- "It's no secret that..."
- "What does this mean for you?"
- "At the end of the day..."
- "The bottom line is..."
- "This is why it's so important..."
- "That's where [X] comes in."
- "This is where [X] plays a crucial role."
- "It's more than just X. It's Y." (the AI contrast formula)
- "[Problem]? Meet [solution]." (the AI solution formula)
- "Unlock the power of..."
- "Revolutionizing the way we..."

**Closing phrases:**
- "In conclusion, breathwork is..."
- "To summarize, we've covered..."
- "By now, you should understand..."
- "I hope this article has helped you..."

### Banned Structural Patterns

- **Symmetrical paragraphs** — every paragraph the same length. Real writing breathes. Some paragraphs are one sentence. Some are six. Vary it.
- **The AI H2 pattern** — headings that all start with the same word or follow the same template: "Understanding X", "The Importance of X", "Benefits of X", "How to X", "Why X Matters". Each heading should be distinct in structure.
- **Keyword-stuffed headings** — repeating the primary keyword in every H2/H3. H1 establishes the keyword; subsequent headings use synonyms and specific angles. *(Paul Taubman critique, confirmed by Dan)*
- **The perfect 3-item list** — AI loves exactly 3 bullet points in perfect grammatical parallel. Real lists have 4, 7, or 2 items and aren't all grammatically identical.
- **Abstract before concrete** — explaining what something IS before showing what it FEELS LIKE. Dan always shows the experience first.
- **Over-explained metaphors** — trust the reader. If you use a metaphor, don't immediately explain it.
- **Passive observation sentences** — "Breathwork has been shown to..." "It has been noted that..." Kill the passive voice.
- **The fake-question H2** — "What Is Conscious Breathing?", "What Are the Benefits?", "How Does It Work?" — sounds like an FAQ robot. Write human section titles.

## Dan Brulé Voice Patterns — Extracted from Approved Content

These patterns come directly from: (1) the approved "5 Mistakes" blog draft, (2) Dan's Pineal Gland rewrite, (3) Anastasia's approved version, (4) Dan's direct email edits, (5) Dan's newsletter emails.

### Pattern 1: The Problem Opens the Article (Not the Definition)

**Wrong (AI):** *"Conscious breathing is the practice of intentionally directing your breath to achieve specific physiological and psychological effects."*

**Right (Dan):** *"A breathwork course can look simple from the outside. You inhale. You exhale. You expect to feel better. And then… you don't."*

The reader must feel the problem before they get the solution.

---

### Pattern 2: Short Sentence Triplets for Emphasis

Three short sentences in a row that build a rhythm, then a longer one that delivers:

*"You inhale. You exhale. You expect to feel better. And then… you don't."*

*"Not through force. Not through mystical shortcuts. But through the simple power of conscious breathing."*

*"Softer inhale. Longer exhale. Slower pace."*

---

### Pattern 3: Sensory Symptoms Before the Fix

Name what the reader physically FEELS before telling them what's wrong:

*"It often feels like:*
*Dry mouth or throat.*
*A rushed, wired feeling.*
*Trouble settling into rhythm."*

Then the fix. Not before. Always after.

---

### Pattern 4: Corrections That Don't Judge

*"Many beginners aren't doing breathwork 'wrong.'"*
*"They're just doing it too much."*

Never say "you're making a mistake." Say "here's what's happening."

---

### Pattern 5: One-Line Wisdom at the End of a Section

After a practical section, one short declarative that feels like a principle:

*"In breathwork, steady usually goes further than intense."*
*"The goal is not to force an experience. The goal is to build a relationship with the breath."*
*"Calm comes from relaxed pacing — not from 'more.'"*

These lines should feel like they could be engraved.

---

### Pattern 6: Parallel Negatives for Contrast

*"Not by force. By invitation."*
*"Not through mystical shortcuts. But through the simple power of conscious breathing."*
*"Not more sensation — more skill."*

---

### Pattern 7: The Ellipsis Pause

Dan uses "…" before a turn or a reveal:

*"And then… you don't."*
*"But then, about a month later… completely out of the blue…"* (newsletter)

Use sparingly — maximum 2 per article. Reserve for genuine dramatic turns.

---

### Pattern 8: Physical Instructions as Short Commands

When giving practice instructions, Dan uses command fragments, not full sentences:

*"Relax the jaw. Let the tongue rest softly on the roof of the mouth. Soften the inhale until the breath becomes quiet again."*

Not: *"You should try to relax your jaw and let your tongue rest on the roof of your mouth."*

---

### Pattern 9: Honest Admission

Dan admits complexity, uncertainty, and limitations:

*"I can't remember exactly what I said now..."*
*"Those can happen. But they are not the point."*
*"What real progress looks like... usually looks much simpler."*

This builds trust more than any credential claim.

---

### Pattern 10: The Telescoping Ending

Dan's posts don't end with a summary. They end by zooming out from the practical to the philosophical, then landing softly:

*"For many people breathwork eventually becomes more than a relaxation technique. It becomes a way of reconnecting with something deeper within themselves... Not because you forced something to happen. But because you learned how to listen."*

Then the CTA. Then the byline.

## Article Structure Templates

**Both templates are written in Dan's voice.** The difference is structural: personal posts flow freely; technique/SEO posts have required sections for search visibility. Dan's personality, vocabulary, and perspective are present in both.

### Template A — Dan's Personal/Devotional Post

1. **Opening moment** — a specific anecdote, current event, world observation, or direct quote that drops the reader into a real situation. No definitions. No background. Start mid-thought.
2. **What this post is about** — one or two sentences, spoken directly. "Today I want to talk about..."
3. **The teaching** — flowing prose, mix of science and spirit, builds progressively. No symmetrical paragraphs. Vary length.
4. **Practice exercises** — embedded naturally in the text. *"Try this right now:"* or *"Here's a simple exercise:"*. Never appended as a separate section.
5. **Lineage reference** — brief. Where this technique comes from. One sentence.
6. **Closing philosophical turn** — zoom from practical to meaningful. 2-4 sentences. No summary.
7. **Invitation** — Practitioner Program, Breathfest, Inner Circle, or upcoming event. Soft, never salesy.
8. **Closing signature** — *"Good luck in your practice, and many blessings on your path."*
9. **Byline** — Dan (Guchu Ram Singh)

*Length: 900-1,800 words. Flow over structure. No strict header requirements.*

### Template B — Dan's SEO/Technique Post

Same voice as Template A, but with structured sections for search visibility. Dan is still speaking — he's just organized his thoughts into a teaching format because the topic calls for it. Think of it as Dan giving a workshop, not writing a love letter.

1. **H1** — primary keyword in title; benefit-forward or problem-forward; never a question H1
2. **Opening hook** — 2-3 sentences in Dan's voice. A relatable problem or a scene from his experience. *"A breathwork course can look simple from the outside. You inhale. You exhale."* No definition, no background.
3. **Quick answer** — 1-2 sentences. Dan cuts straight to what matters. Readers who just want the answer get it; readers who want Dan's full teaching keep reading.
4. **Section: Why This Works** — Dan explains the mechanism (ANS, vagus nerve, HRV, CO2) in his approachable way. *"Here's what's happening in your body..."* 1 paragraph. No academic language.
5. **Section(s): The Technique(s)** — Dan's physical commands. Short sentences. Sensory descriptions. *"Relax the jaw. Let the tongue rest softly on the roof of the mouth."*
6. **Section: Common Mistakes** — Dan diagnoses what the reader is doing wrong, gently. *"Many beginners aren't doing breathwork 'wrong.' They're just doing too much."* Physical sensation descriptions before fixes.
7. **Section: Safety Note** — consolidated here, once only. Dan's caring authority. Not repeated elsewhere.
8. **Section: FAQ** — 5+ Q&A pairs. Dan answers directly — real opinions, not hedging. *"Can you do too much breathwork? Absolutely. I've seen it hundreds of times."*
9. **CTA** — Dan's invitation. Soft, personal. *"If you're ready to go deeper..."*
10. **Closing signature** — *"Good luck in your practice, and many blessings on your path."*
11. **Byline** — Dan (Guchu Ram Singh)
12. **References** — 3-5 named sources at bottom (no inline citations).

*Length: 1,400-2,200 words. Headers, lists, and tables required. FAQ required.*

**H2 heading rules for Template B:**
- H1 contains primary keyword once
- First H2 uses a related phrase (NOT the exact keyword again)
- Subsequent H2s use specific angles, synonyms, questions, or scenario-based phrasing
- NO H2 should start with "Understanding X", "The Importance of X", or "Benefits of X"
- Example for topic "coherent breathing":
  - H1: *"Coherent Breathing: A Practical Guide to Slowing Down and Coming Home"*
  - H2: *"What 5 Breaths Per Minute Does to Your Nervous System"*
  - H2: *"How to Practice — Step by Step"*
  - H2: *"The Six Bridges: Where to Relax While You Breathe"*
  - H2: *"Common Mistakes That Turn This Practice Into Work"*
  - H2: *"FAQ"*

## SEO Technical Rules

*(Translated from SEO Writing Standards document)*

### Keyword Density
- Primary keyword: **~3% of total words** (minimum 4 times per 300 words)
- Must appear in: H1, at least 2 H2s, and body text
- Form/case may be adjusted minimally (plural, verb form) — prefer original form
- Long-tail phrases: appear 2–3 times maximum in body; use in or near original form

### Headings
- H1: required, contains primary keyword, minimum 2 words
- H2: minimum 2 per article; each must relate to content beneath it
- H2s should contain primary keyword or a strong related term
- H3/H4: use when needed; don't force
- All headings: minimum 2 words; no single-word headings

### Sentence Length
- Target: under 20 words per sentence
- Exceptions: 2–3 longer sentences per section are acceptable when needed for flow
- Shorter is better for readability and indexing

### Paragraph Length
- Maximum 250 words per section (under one H2)
- If a section exceeds 250 words, split with an additional H2 or H3

### Internal Links
- Minimum 3–5 internal links per post
- Link relevant terms and technique names to related posts or pages
- Required: always link to `/practitioner-program/` in professional-development posts
- Required: always link to the 21-Day Training opt-in in beginner posts
- Required: always link to `/events/` when mentioning live training or events
- **DO NOT link to** `/product-category/training-programs/` — this is a 404 page (March 2026)

### External Links
- 2–3 per post minimum; link to named, high-quality sources
- Preferred: Stanford, NHS, Cleveland Clinic, NCCIH, HeartMath Institute, Columbia University Medical
- External links must add genuine value for the reader; not just credibility theater

### CTA
- Every article must have at least one clear CTA
- CTA placement: end of article, and optionally mid-article after the practice exercise
- CTA tone: invitational, never pressured (see BRAND.md for CTA hierarchy)

### Hub-and-Spoke / Cornerstone Structure
- Every technique post should link to a broader "what is breathwork" or "how to start" cornerstone
- Each topic cluster should have one cornerstone article (the deepest, most comprehensive)
- All posts within a cluster link back to the cornerstone
- Posts in the same cluster link to each other where natural
- Cornerstone candidates: Conscious Breathing (Two Pillars), Breathwork for Beginners, Breathwork for Anxiety, Spiritual Breathing

## Do

- **Start mid-scene or mid-problem** — never with background or definition
- **Vary sentence length radically** — a one-word sentence. Then a flowing sentence that takes the reader from where they are to where you want them. Then two short punches.
- **Show the sensation before explaining the mechanism** — what does it feel like in the body, first
- **Use Dan's exact approved phrases** — they are the voice; see Coined Phrases above
- **Include one embedded practice exercise** — readers must be able to do something right now
- **Reference Dan's lineage at least once** — 300,000 people, 73 countries, 50+ years; it's the credibility anchor
- **Use rhetorical questions to invite** — not to lecture or introduce sections
- **Honor both science and spirit in every post** — never sacrifice one for the other
- **One consolidated safety note** — not scattered; once is enough
- **Earn every transition** — if you're moving to a new idea, show why with a concrete detail or observation, not a transition word

## Don't

- Don't start with definitions, background, or "Breathing is..."
- Don't use any of the banned words or phrases (see Anti-AI section)
- Don't use intensity/force language: "push through," "challenge yourself," "warrior mindset"
- Don't keyword-stuff headings — H1 establishes the keyword; vary all subsequent headings
- Don't write for search engines first — Paul Taubman's rule, confirmed by Dan: *"Write for the reader, not the search engines"*
- Don't scatter safety disclaimers across multiple sections — Dan removed scattered safety refs from his own rewrite
- Don't over-science experiential topics — pineal gland, third eye, Still Point, energy breathing: grounded but not clinical
- Don't write posts without a practice exercise — breathwork is experiential, not theoretical
- Don't use generic wellness language: "boost your wellness," "enhance your health," "optimize your well-being"
- Don't position Dan as just a teacher — he is a lineage holder, practitioner, and missionary for the field
- Don't end with "In conclusion..." or a summary — end with a philosophical turn, then CTA
- Don't use symmetrical paragraphs — vary length in every section

## Science Citation Style

**Approachable, not academic.** Reference organization name + key finding. Skip APA/MLA:

> "A 2022 randomized study from Stanford found that just 5 minutes of cyclic sighing daily reduced anxiety and improved mood more effectively than any other breathing pattern tested."

Not: "(Smith et al., 2022, Cell Reports Medicine, 3(1), DOI: 10.1016/j.xcrm.2022.100895)"

Always follow the science mention with a single practical sentence about what it means for the reader.
