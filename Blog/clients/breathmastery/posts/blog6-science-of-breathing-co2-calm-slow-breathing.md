# Science of Breathing: CO2, Calm, and Why Slow Breathing Works So Fast

Take a slow breath right now. Make the exhale longer than the inhale.

Something just happened in your body. Your heart rate dropped slightly. The muscles around your shoulders and jaw softened. If you were mid-conversation, you probably became a slightly better listener.

That shift took about twelve seconds. No medication. No equipment. Just a change in breathing rhythm.

The question is: why? What exactly happens in the body when you slow your breath down? Why does the exhale matter more than the inhale? And why does the effect feel almost immediate when it should, by most people's intuition, take longer?

The science behind this is more elegant — and more practical — than most people realize. Understanding it changes how you use breathing deliberately.

---

## The Molecule Most People Get Wrong: CO2

Most people think of oxygen as the goal of breathing. Breathe deeply, get more oxygen in, feel better.

The reality is more specific, and more interesting.

Carbon dioxide (CO2) — which most people think of as a waste product to be expelled — is actually one of the primary regulators of breathing rate and nervous system state. The urge to breathe is not triggered by low oxygen. It is triggered by rising CO2.

When CO2 rises in the bloodstream, chemoreceptors in the brainstem and the carotid arteries signal the respiratory center to breathe faster and deeper. When CO2 falls — as it does during hyperventilation — those signals decrease and blood vessels constrict. This is why overbreathing can produce light-headedness, tingling in the hands, and a paradoxical sense of air hunger even when oxygen is plentiful.

The Bohr Effect adds another layer. Hemoglobin, the protein that carries oxygen in the bloodstream, releases oxygen to the tissues more readily in the presence of CO2 and mild acidity. Over-breathing reduces CO2, which paradoxically reduces oxygen delivery to the cells — the opposite of what most people intend when they "breathe deeply."

This is one reason Dan Brulé has taught for decades that the goal is not more breathing. It's better breathing. Aware breathing. Conscious breathing.

## The Autonomic Nervous System and the Exhale

The autonomic nervous system (ANS) has two primary branches. The sympathetic branch activates fight-or-flight: heart rate rises, muscles ready, digestion pauses, attention narrows. The parasympathetic branch activates rest-and-restore: heart rate slows, digestion resumes, muscles release.

These two branches are in constant dynamic balance. Breathing is one of the most direct levers for shifting that balance — because breathing is both involuntary and voluntary. You can watch it happen on its own, or you can direct it consciously.

Here is the key mechanism:

During inhalation, the diaphragm descends, expanding the chest cavity. This creates a brief drop in pressure that slightly increases heart rate. During exhalation, the diaphragm rises, pressure increases, and heart rate slows.

This oscillation — heart rate rising on the inhale and falling on the exhale — is called respiratory sinus arrhythmia, and the magnitude of that oscillation is a direct measure of vagal tone. A higher amplitude means a more responsive, more regulated nervous system.

The vagus nerve is the primary conduit of the parasympathetic nervous system. It runs from the brainstem through the neck, chest, and abdomen, carrying signals in both directions between the brain and the body. Slow, rhythmic breathing stimulates vagal tone. Research from the Cleveland Clinic and HeartMath Institute has consistently shown that controlled breathing increases heart rate variability (HRV) — a validated biomarker of autonomic nervous system balance and resilience.

The practical implication: a longer exhale produces greater parasympathetic activation than a shorter one. This is why "exhale longer than the inhale" is not a belief — it is a measurable physiological event.

## What Slow Breathing Does to the Brain

A 2023 study published in *Cell Reports Medicine* (Stanford University) compared three different breathing techniques in a randomized trial: cyclic sighing (double inhale followed by a long exhale), box breathing, and cyclic hyperventilation. Cyclic sighing — which emphasizes the long exhale — produced the greatest improvements in mood and the greatest reductions in physiological stress markers.

The study is significant because it used controlled methodology and objective measures. It is also consistent with what practitioners have observed in clinical and community settings for decades.

Slow breathing — approximately five to six breaths per minute — has been associated in peer-reviewed research with:

- Increased heart rate variability (HeartMath Institute; multiple peer-reviewed studies)
- Reduced cortisol and inflammatory markers (Columbia University Medical Center)
- Improved cognitive performance and working memory
- Reduced self-reported anxiety and depressive symptoms (NCCIH review, 2021)
- Enhanced immune function in longitudinal breathwork practitioners

Dr. Patricia Gerbarg and Dr. Richard Brown at Columbia University Medical Center have conducted extensive research on yogic breathing and mental health, finding significant reductions in anxiety and PTSD symptoms from coherent breathing practices.

None of this is new knowledge. Ancient traditions — Pranayama in Yoga, breathing practices in Taoist Chi Kung, meditative breath regulation in Zen — arrived at similar conclusions through thousands of years of direct experimentation. Modern physiology is largely confirming what practitioners have known experientially for generations.

## Coherent Breathing: When Biology and Ancient Practice Align

Coherent Breathing, a term coined by researcher Stephen Elliott in his 2005 book *The New Science of Breath*, refers to breathing at approximately five breaths per minute — roughly five to six seconds inhale, five to six seconds exhale.

At this rhythm, the breath oscillation synchronizes with the natural resonance frequency of the cardiovascular system. Heart rate variability amplitude increases substantially. The interaction between the respiratory and cardiovascular systems reaches a kind of efficiency.

Elliott's research, and subsequent work by Dr. David O'Hare in *365 Heart Coherence*, found that regular practice at this resonance frequency produced lasting improvements in vagal tone and HRV — not just during the practice itself, but as a baseline measure over time.

Coherent Breathing is not a breathwork technique invented recently. It maps closely onto what Pranayama teachers describe as the balanced, rhythmic breath used in meditation. What modern research did was measure what was already being practiced.

## The Physiology of the Sigh

The physiological sigh is something the body does spontaneously — a double inhale followed by an extended exhale that reinflates collapsed alveoli (air sacs in the lungs) and clears CO2 more efficiently than a normal breath.

Dr. Andrew Huberman's lab at Stanford popularized this technique in recent years, and the 2023 *Cell Reports Medicine* study attached measurable data to it. But the basic mechanism — a long exhale that deeply engages the parasympathetic response — has been described in yogic breathing traditions for centuries.

The practical version: two short inhales through the nose (the second one "tops off" the lungs), followed by a slow, complete exhale through the mouth or nose. Done once, it produces a rapid state shift. Done in cycles for five minutes, it is one of the most effective tools currently documented for acute stress relief.

Dan Brulé has taught versions of this pattern for decades — not because of the Stanford study, but because it works, and because the yogic traditions he trained in recognized this long before anyone put it in a journal.

## The Nose vs. the Mouth

One distinction that matters more than most people realize: whether you breathe through your nose or your mouth.

Nasal breathing produces nitric oxide, a vasodilator that improves oxygen uptake in the lungs. It also filters, warms, and humidifies incoming air. Nasal breathing is slower by nature — the narrow passages resist airflow — which naturally reduces breathing rate.

Mouth breathing is faster and less regulated. Chronic mouth breathing is associated with sleep disruption, reduced CO2 tolerance, and a range of downstream physiological effects that researchers like Patrick McKeown have documented extensively.

For stress-relief breathing, nose breathing on both inhale and exhale is preferable when possible. The resistance itself is part of the benefit.

## A Practice Built on the Science

Here is a five-minute sequence that applies these mechanisms directly.

**Minute 1:** Observe only. Close your eyes. Notice your current breathing pattern without changing it. Fast or slow? Shallow or deep? Nose or mouth?

**Minutes 2–3: Coherent Breathing.** Inhale through the nose for five to six seconds. Exhale through the nose for five to six seconds. Aim for the smoothest possible rhythm — no pauses, no effort, continuous and even. Feel your chest and belly expand gently on the inhale and release fully on the exhale.

**Minutes 4–5: Extended Exhale.** Now lengthen the exhale. Inhale for four counts, exhale for six to eight counts. Let the exhale be slow and complete — not forced, just extended.

If at any point you feel dizzy or uncomfortable, return to normal breathing and rest. Both sensations are data: dizziness often means CO2 is dropping (you may be breathing a bit too forcefully), and discomfort may mean the pace is too aggressive.

Soften the inhale until the breath becomes quiet again. Calm comes from relaxed pacing — not from effort.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why does breathing slowly reduce stress so quickly when other stress interventions take longer?**

Breathing acts directly on the autonomic nervous system through the vagus nerve, which means the shift is physiological, not just psychological. You're not thinking your way to calm — you're activating a different branch of the nervous system. The parasympathetic response begins within the first few breaths of a slower rhythm. No other behavioral intervention has such a direct and immediate pathway to ANS state change.

**Is CO2 actually good for you? I thought we breathe to get rid of it.**

CO2 is not simply a waste product. It is a primary regulator of breathing rate, a key signal in oxygen delivery to the cells (the Bohr Effect), and a vasodilator that controls blood flow. The goal is optimal CO2 levels — not as low as possible. Chronic overbreathing reduces CO2 below ideal levels, which can contribute to anxiety, light-headedness, poor sleep, and reduced athletic performance. Breathing less, but better, is often the correct direction.

**What is heart rate variability (HRV) and why does it matter?**

HRV is the variation in time between consecutive heartbeats. A higher HRV indicates a more adaptive, responsive autonomic nervous system — one that can shift quickly between activation and recovery. Low HRV is associated with chronic stress, cardiovascular risk, and reduced psychological resilience. Controlled breathing, particularly at resonance frequency (about five breaths per minute), is one of the most effective non-pharmacological ways to increase HRV.

**How does coherent breathing differ from standard "deep breathing"?**

Coherent breathing is not about breathing deeply — it's about breathing at a specific rhythm (approximately five breaths per minute) that synchronizes the cardiovascular and respiratory systems. "Deep breathing" as a vague instruction often leads people to force large, fast breaths that can actually reduce CO2 and increase sympathetic activation. Coherent breathing is slower, more regular, and aimed at resonance rather than volume.

**How long before breathwork produces lasting changes in stress resilience, not just short-term relief?**

Research and practitioner experience both suggest that consistent daily practice over four to eight weeks produces measurable changes in baseline HRV, cortisol reactivity, and self-reported stress levels. Short-term practices (a few minutes when stressed) work immediately but don't build the structural changes. Regular practice — even five to ten minutes daily — is where the lasting physiological shifts develop.
