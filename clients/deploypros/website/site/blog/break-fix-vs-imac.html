Break/Fix vs. IMAC: Which Field Service Model Fits Your Operation?

March 21, 2026 5 min read

If you manage IT infrastructure across multiple locations, you have almost certainly dealt with both break/fix and IMAC work. But many operations managers treat these as interchangeable categories of "field service" without recognizing the fundamental differences in planning, staffing, cost structure, and vendor management that each model requires.

Understanding these differences matters because the wrong service model applied to the wrong situation leads to overspending, missed SLAs, and frustrated end users. Here is a clear-eyed breakdown of both models and when each one makes sense.

Defining Break/Fix

Break/fix is reactive field service. Something fails, and a technician is dispatched to repair or replace it. The trigger is always an incident: a POS terminal goes down in a retail store, a network switch fails at a branch office, a workstation stops booting at a healthcare clinic.

The defining characteristics of break/fix work are urgency and unpredictability. You cannot plan a break/fix visit three weeks in advance because you do not know what will break or when. The service level agreement is built around response time, typically measured in hours. A 4-hour SLA means a qualified technician must be on site within 4 hours of the trouble ticket being created.

Break/fix technicians need to be diagnosticians. They arrive at a site not knowing exactly what they will find. The POS terminal might need a power supply swap, a full unit replacement, a network cable reseat, or a software reconfiguration. The technician must carry a broad skill set and a flexible parts kit because the scope is defined by whatever they discover on site.

From a cost perspective, break/fix is priced per incident. Each dispatch has a fixed cost (the truck roll fee) plus variable costs for parts and labor beyond a baseline. Companies with high break/fix volume often negotiate rate cards with their field service providers that define per-incident pricing by service type and geography.

Defining IMAC

IMAC stands for Install, Move, Add, Change, and it represents planned lifecycle management of IT assets. Unlike break/fix, IMAC work is proactive. It is scheduled in advance, scoped in detail, and executed against a defined project plan.

Install covers net-new equipment deployment. Setting up workstations for a new office, deploying kiosks across a retail chain, or installing access points for a wireless network buildout. The equipment is procured, staged, and shipped to the site before the technician arrives.

Move handles physical relocation of existing IT assets. Office consolidations, floor plan changes, and site migrations. The technician disconnects equipment at the origin, transports it (or coordinates with logistics for larger moves), and reinstalls it at the destination with full connectivity verification.

Add covers incremental expansion to existing infrastructure. Adding a monitor to a workstation, installing a secondary printer, or connecting a new peripheral to an existing network drop. These are typically lower-complexity tasks that can be bundled into a single site visit with other IMAC work.

Change encompasses configuration updates, hardware upgrades, and technology refreshes. Swapping a laptop for a newer model, upgrading RAM, replacing an aging switch with a current-generation unit. Change work often runs on refresh cycles, with organizations replacing a percentage of their fleet each quarter or year.

IMAC pricing is project-based rather than incident-based. The scope is defined upfront, the timeline is agreed upon, and the cost is either a fixed project fee or a per-site rate multiplied by the number of locations.

Key Differences That Matter

Reactive vs. proactive. Break/fix responds to failures. IMAC prevents them or manages the natural lifecycle of equipment before failure occurs. This fundamental difference drives every downstream decision about staffing, scheduling, and cost management.

SLA structure. Break/fix SLAs measure response time to unplanned events. IMAC SLAs measure on-time completion of scheduled work. A break/fix provider that responds within 4 hours is meeting their obligation. An IMAC provider that completes 95% of scheduled sites on the planned date is meeting theirs. Different metrics, different incentive structures.

Cost models. Break/fix costs are variable and difficult to forecast because incident volume is unpredictable. IMAC costs are fixed and budgetable because the scope is defined in advance. Organizations that shift more of their field service spend toward planned IMAC work gain better cost visibility and reduce the financial volatility of reactive break/fix incidents.

Technician skill profile. Break/fix demands broad diagnostic ability and adaptability. The technician does not know what they will encounter. IMAC demands procedural precision and speed. The technician executes a defined playbook at each site. Some technicians excel at both; many are stronger in one mode than the other. Staffing decisions should account for this distinction.

When Break/Fix Is the Right Model

Break/fix is appropriate when the equipment base is unpredictable, failures are sporadic, and the cost of downtime exceeds the cost of maintaining rapid-response capability. Common scenarios include:

Legacy infrastructure that is past its warranty period and beyond the reach of manufacturer support. When a 12-year-old POS terminal fails, you need someone who can diagnose and repair aging hardware rather than follow a new-installation playbook.

Emergency support for critical systems where any downtime has immediate revenue or operational impact. A downed network switch at a financial trading floor, a failed payment terminal during peak retail hours, or a broken HVAC controller in a data center. These situations demand fast response, not scheduled project management.

Geographically dispersed assets where failure rates are low but response coverage is still needed. A company with 200 branch offices that each experience one or two hardware incidents per year cannot justify a standing IMAC contract but needs break/fix coverage for when problems occur.

When IMAC Is the Right Model

IMAC is the right model when the work is plannable, the scope is definable, and the organization benefits from batch execution rather than one-off responses.

Technology refresh cycles are the most common IMAC use case. Replacing 5,000 laptops across 120 offices over a 6-month window is pure IMAC work. The scope is known, the timeline is fixed, and the execution follows a repeatable process at each site.

Office moves and consolidations involve relocating entire IT environments from one physical space to another. The complexity is in coordination, not diagnosis. Every asset needs to be inventoried, disconnected, transported, reconnected, and verified. IMAC project management ensures nothing gets lost in the shuffle.

New deployments for expanding organizations. Opening 30 new retail locations, building out a new corporate campus, or deploying a new POS platform across all stores. These are greenfield installations with defined hardware lists, standard configurations, and scheduled cutover dates.

The Hybrid Reality

In practice, most field service operations need both models running simultaneously. A retail chain executing a 500-store POS refresh (IMAC) will still experience hardware failures at stores that have already been upgraded or stores still waiting in the queue (break/fix). An enterprise completing an office move (IMAC) will still need reactive support for network issues that surface after the migration (break/fix).

The operational challenge is managing both service streams through a single coordination layer. The IMAC project team is tracking scheduled sites, equipment staging, and daily completion targets. The break/fix team is managing a queue of unplanned incidents with response-time SLAs. Both teams draw from the same technician pool and compete for the same dispatch resources.

Organizations that manage this dual-model operation well typically use a tiered staffing approach: dedicated technicians for the IMAC rollout (who follow the project schedule) and a separate flex pool for break/fix response (who handle incidents as they arrive). The AI coordination layer manages both streams, confirming IMAC technicians for their scheduled sites while also tracking break/fix response times and technician availability.

How AI Optimizes Both Models

AI confirmation and dispatch intelligence apply to both break/fix and IMAC work, though the optimization targets differ.

For break/fix, AI improves response time by pre-qualifying available technicians based on location, skill set, and current workload. When an incident comes in, the system identifies the best-fit technician and initiates confirmation within minutes rather than waiting for a dispatcher to work through their contact list.

For IMAC, AI improves execution reliability by confirming every scheduled technician, detecting risk signals early, and triggering backup protocols before missed appointments derail the project timeline. On a 200-site rollout, the difference between a 90% and 99% daily confirmation rate is the difference between constant rescheduling and smooth execution.

Making the Decision

The decision between break/fix and IMAC is not either/or. It is a question of proportion and priority. Ask these questions about your current operation:

What percentage of your field service spend goes to reactive incidents versus planned work? If more than 40% of your budget is reactive, there may be opportunities to shift to proactive lifecycle management through IMAC programs.

How predictable is your failure rate? If you can forecast incident volume within 20%, you can right-size your break/fix coverage. If failures are highly unpredictable, you need more break/fix flexibility.

Are you running any technology refresh, office move, or new deployment projects in the next 12 months? If so, those should be scoped and managed as IMAC work with dedicated project management, not fed into the break/fix queue.

The field service operations that perform best are the ones that clearly categorize their work, apply the right service model to each category, and use technology to coordinate both streams without letting either one starve the other for resources.
