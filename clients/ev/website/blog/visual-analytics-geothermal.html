The Role of Visual Analytics in Geothermal Energy Production

By EV Technical Team March 15, 2026 7 min read

Geothermal energy is experiencing a global resurgence. Governments and operators are investing heavily in both conventional hydrothermal systems and next-generation enhanced geothermal systems (EGS) as the push toward carbon-free baseload power intensifies. Yet geothermal wells present some of the most aggressive operating conditions on the planet, and the challenges that come with those conditions have historically been difficult to diagnose without pulling completion equipment to surface.

Visual analytics changes that equation. By deploying high-temperature downhole cameras and applying computational image analysis, operators gain direct, quantified insight into what is happening inside their wells, often revealing problems that conventional logging tools miss entirely.

The Persistent Challenges in Geothermal Wells

Geothermal wells operate in environments that would destroy most oilfield instrumentation. Temperatures regularly exceed 200 degrees Celsius. The brine chemistry is often highly acidic or saturated with dissolved minerals. And production intervals can span hundreds of meters of open hole or slotted liner.

Three issues dominate geothermal well integrity and production optimization discussions:

  • Mineral scaling. Silica, calcite, and sulfide deposits accumulate on casing walls, inside perforations, and across downhole equipment. Left unchecked, scaling reduces flow area, restricts production, and can eventually block a well entirely.
  • Corrosion. Aggressive brine chemistry attacks casing, liner hangers, and completion hardware. The rate and distribution of corrosion determines remaining casing life and intervention planning.
  • Declining injectivity. Injection wells suffer from particulate accumulation, biological fouling, and fracture face damage. When injectivity drops, the entire production loop is affected.

Standard mechanical and electronic logging tools can measure temperature, pressure, and flow rate. They can detect gross casing damage through calipers and magnetic flux. But they cannot show an operator exactly what scaling pattern is forming, where corrosion pitting is most severe, or whether an obstruction is mineral, biological, or mechanical in origin.

What Visual Analytics Brings to Geothermal Operations

Visual analytics combines high-definition downhole video with computational image processing to produce quantified assessments from visual data. Rather than relying on a human observer to interpret footage in real time, visual analytics algorithms measure specific features: the percentage of casing wall covered by scale, the depth and distribution pattern of corrosion pitting, the size and shape of foreign objects or obstructions.

This approach delivers three things that conventional tools cannot:

Direct evidence. Video footage provides unambiguous visual confirmation of downhole conditions. When a scaled section is identified through temperature or pressure anomalies, visual analytics confirms the nature, extent, and distribution of the deposit. Operators make remediation decisions based on direct observation rather than inference.

Quantified measurement from visual data. EV's visual analytics products convert camera footage into measured parameters. Corrosion VA, for example, produces severity mapping across the inspected interval. Scale VA quantifies deposit coverage as a percentage of available flow area. These are not qualitative assessments; they are measurements that feed directly into asset management models.

Baseline comparison over time. When a well is surveyed at regular intervals, visual analytics produces time-lapse comparisons that show how conditions are evolving. Is scaling accelerating after a chemistry change? Has a corrosion treatment reduced pitting rates? Visual analytics provides the evidence to answer these questions definitively.

EV's Track Record in Geothermal Environments

EV has operated in geothermal wells across multiple continents. The Basis R200 camera system, rated to 200 degrees Celsius and 1,034 bar, was designed specifically for high-temperature applications where standard camera systems would fail within minutes. The system delivers monochrome video at frame rates sufficient for visual analytics processing even in extreme thermal environments.

Across these deployments, the most consistent finding is that visual analytics identifies conditions that other tools do not detect. Scaling patterns often develop in locations that temperature and pressure surveys do not flag. Corrosion frequently concentrates at specific metallurgical boundaries or completion junctions that calipers cannot resolve with sufficient precision.

ClearVision: Combining Video with Ultrasound

EV's ClearVision technology integrates array video with phased array ultrasound in a single tool string. For geothermal operators, this combination is significant because it provides both surface condition assessment (through video and visual analytics) and wall thickness measurement (through ultrasound) in a single run.

The result is a complete picture: operators see where corrosion is occurring on the inner surface and simultaneously measure how much wall thickness remains. This combined dataset supports casing integrity assessments, remaining life calculations, and risk-based inspection planning in a way that neither technology could achieve independently.

The Future of Visual Analytics in Geothermal

As geothermal drilling targets deeper, hotter reservoirs, the demands on inspection technology will increase. Enhanced geothermal systems operating at 300 degrees Celsius or above will require instrumentation that pushes current temperature boundaries. The integration of visual analytics with machine learning classifiers is already underway, with the goal of automated defect detection and severity grading at the wellsite.

EV's AIVA platform provides the digital infrastructure to manage visual analytics data across entire geothermal fields. Rather than treating each well survey as an isolated event, AIVA enables field-wide trend analysis, predictive maintenance scheduling, and centralized reporting for regulatory compliance.

The direction is clear: geothermal operators who adopt visual analytics gain a material advantage in well integrity management, production optimization, and asset life extension. The technology exists today, and its application in geothermal environments is growing steadily.
