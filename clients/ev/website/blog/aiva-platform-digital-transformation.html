AIVA: How Digital Platforms Are Transforming Well Integrity Management

By EV Technical Team February 10, 2026 6 min read

The oil and gas industry has talked about digital transformation for a decade. In well integrity management, that conversation is moving from aspiration to operational reality. Operators who once managed inspection data across disconnected spreadsheets, email chains, and shared drives are migrating to centralized platforms that consolidate data, automate reporting, and enable team-wide collaboration on well condition assessments.

EV's AIVA (Asset Inspection, Visualisation & Analytics) platform represents this shift. Built specifically for the inspection and well integrity domain, AIVA addresses the data management problems that have plagued operators for years, while adding visualization and analytics capabilities that standalone tools cannot replicate.

The Problem with Traditional Data Management

Well integrity data has historically been fragmented by design. Each inspection run produces its own dataset: video files, log data, caliper surveys, corrosion maps, and analyst reports. These datasets are generated by different service companies, in different formats, at different times. The operator's challenge is to assemble these disparate inputs into a coherent picture of well condition.

In practice, this assembly process is manual and error-prone. Inspection reports sit in email inboxes. Video files are stored on portable drives. Comparative analysis between inspection campaigns requires manually locating, loading, and aligning datasets from different sources and different years. The institutional knowledge about a specific well's history often resides in the memory of individual engineers rather than in an accessible system.

The consequences of this fragmentation are real and measurable. Decisions about intervention timing, inspection frequency, and well retirement are made with incomplete data because assembling the complete picture takes too long. Regulatory reporting consumes engineering hours that would be better spent on analysis. When key personnel leave or change roles, knowledge about well condition history goes with them.

What AIVA Changes

AIVA consolidates all inspection data for a well, a field, or an entire portfolio into a single, secure, cloud-hosted platform. The system is built around four functional pillars:

Secure, centralized storage. All inspection data, regardless of source or format, lives in one place. Video footage, log data, analyst reports, and visual analytics outputs are stored against the specific well and specific inspection campaign that generated them. Role-based access controls ensure that data is available to authorized users and protected from unauthorized access.

Real-time collaboration. Multiple users can access, review, and annotate inspection data simultaneously. An engineer in Aberdeen can review the same visual analytics output as a colleague in Houston, with annotations and notes visible to both in real time. This eliminates the delays inherent in emailing files and waiting for responses.

3D visualization. AIVA renders downhole data in a three-dimensional well model. Rather than scrolling through a flat video file or reviewing a tabular corrosion report, engineers can navigate the well in 3D, zooming into specific intervals, rotating the view, and overlaying multiple data types (video, caliper, wall thickness) on the same model. This spatial context makes patterns visible that flat presentations obscure.

Automated reporting. AIVA generates standardized inspection reports from stored data, reducing the manual effort required for regulatory submissions and internal reporting. Report templates are configurable to match operator-specific or regulator-specific requirements, and reports can be generated for individual wells or across entire portfolios.

Integration with Visual Analytics Data

AIVA was designed to work with EV's visual analytics products. When a Corrosion VA, Scale VA, or any other visual analytics assessment is completed, the quantified output feeds directly into AIVA. This means that the measured severity of corrosion, the percentage coverage of scale, or the dimensions of a restriction are automatically associated with the correct well, the correct depth interval, and the correct inspection campaign.

Over time, this creates a longitudinal dataset that shows how well condition is evolving. Each inspection campaign adds another data point. Trend analysis becomes straightforward because the data is structured, time-stamped, and location-referenced consistently across all campaigns.

For operators managing large well portfolios, this longitudinal perspective is particularly valuable. AIVA can surface wells where degradation rates are accelerating, flag wells approaching integrity thresholds, and support risk-based prioritization of inspection and intervention programs across the portfolio.

Where AIVA Is Heading

The AIVA development roadmap is focused on expanding analytics capabilities and deepening integration with third-party data sources. Machine learning classifiers trained on EV's 25-year library of downhole video are being developed to automate feature detection and severity grading. Integration with SCADA systems, production databases, and other operational platforms will enrich the well integrity picture with operational context.

The broader trajectory is toward predictive well integrity management: using historical inspection data, production trends, and operational parameters to forecast when a well will require intervention, rather than discovering the need during a scheduled inspection. AIVA provides the data foundation on which that predictive capability will be built.

For operators still managing inspection data through manual processes, the transition to a platform like AIVA represents a step change in capability. The data exists. The question is whether it is organized and accessible in a way that supports faster, better-informed decisions about well integrity and asset management.
