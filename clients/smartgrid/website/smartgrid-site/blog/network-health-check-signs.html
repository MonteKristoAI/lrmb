5 Warning Signs Your Network Infrastructure Needs a Health Check

Best Practices · January 20, 2026 · 6 min read

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Progressively slower file transfers and application load times often indicate oversubscribed uplinks or aging cabling that cannot sustain current traffic demands.
  • ✓ Two or more unplanned outages per quarter point to systemic hardware, redundancy, or configuration drift problems rather than isolated incidents.
  • ✓ Network documentation that no longer matches physical reality makes troubleshooting slower and capacity planning unreliable.
  • ✓ Wireless networks designed before 2020 were built for a fraction of the device density and video traffic they carry today.
  • ✓ End-of-life hardware running unsupported firmware creates security exposure, compliance risk, and accelerating failure rates.

Network infrastructure does not fail all at once. It degrades in small increments that are easy to dismiss individually but compound into serious operational problems over time. The switches that were top-of-line five years ago are now bottlenecking 10-gigabit uplinks. Cabling installed during the original buildout has been patched, extended, and re-routed so many times that nobody is entirely sure what connects to what. The wireless network was designed for 30 devices per access point, but employees now carry three devices each and IoT sensors have doubled the total device count.

The result is a network that mostly works but occasionally fails in ways that are expensive to diagnose and disruptive to fix. Below are five warning signs that your infrastructure is overdue for a formal health check.

1. File Transfers and Application Load Times Keep Getting Slower

Users rarely report network slowness until it becomes painful enough to pick up the phone. By the time your helpdesk starts fielding complaints about slow file server access or laggy cloud applications, the underlying issue has usually been building for months. Common culprits include oversubscribed uplinks between access and distribution switches, aging Cat 5e cabling that cannot sustain gigabit speeds reliably, and access points running on congested 2.4 GHz channels because the 5 GHz radio was disabled to work around interference problems nobody remembers creating.

A proper network assessment measures actual throughput at every link in the chain, identifies bottlenecks, and maps each one to a specific hardware limitation or configuration issue. The difference between a 15-minute speed test and a professional assessment is the difference between treating symptoms and finding root causes.

2. Unplanned Outages Are Happening More Than Twice a Quarter

Occasional outages are part of running any network. Hardware fails, ISPs have service disruptions, and power events take down equipment. But when unplanned outages start happening more than twice per quarter, something systemic is at play. Frequent outages typically trace back to one of three root causes: aging hardware approaching or past its rated lifespan, inadequate redundancy in the network design, or configuration drift where years of ad-hoc changes have introduced single points of failure that did not exist in the original architecture.

Each unplanned outage carries direct costs in lost productivity and indirect costs in IT staff time spent on reactive troubleshooting instead of planned improvements. A Gartner estimate puts average downtime costs for mid-market companies at roughly $5,600 per minute. Even if your organization sits well below that figure, the cumulative cost of recurring outages across a 12-month period adds up faster than most IT directors realize.

3. Your Network Documentation No Longer Matches Reality

Pull up your network diagram. Walk into the server room. Compare the two. In the majority of organizations we assess, they do not match. Switches have been added without updating the diagram. Patch panels have been re-terminated without updating labels. VLANs exist in the configuration that nobody on the current team can explain because the engineer who created them left the company three years ago.

This documentation gap is not just an administrative inconvenience. It makes troubleshooting slower because engineers cannot trust the diagram to reflect actual connectivity. It increases the risk of misconfigurations during change windows because the starting state is unclear. And it makes accurate capacity planning nearly impossible because you are forecasting growth against a baseline that does not reflect reality.

4. Your Wireless Network Was Designed for a Different Era

If your wireless network was deployed before 2020, it was engineered for a world that no longer exists. Device density has increased dramatically as employees carry phones, laptops, and tablets simultaneously. Video conferencing traffic has exploded since remote and hybrid work became standard. IoT devices ranging from smart thermostats to occupancy sensors and badge readers have added hundreds of connections that were never part of the original access point density calculations.

A modern wireless assessment goes beyond checking signal strength with a free phone app. It includes heat mapping with current device loads, channel utilization analysis across both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands, roaming behavior analysis for mobile devices, and a comparison of your access point density and placement against current best practices for your specific environment type, whether that is an open-plan office, a warehouse, a healthcare facility, or a retail floor.

5. You Are Running End-of-Life Hardware or Firmware

Every piece of network hardware has a published end-of-life date and an end-of-support date. Equipment past its support window does not receive security patches, cannot be replaced under warranty if it fails, and may not support the latest protocols and features your organization needs. We regularly find enterprises running switches with firmware from 2018 that carries known vulnerabilities, or access points that cannot support WPA3 because the hardware predates the standard entirely.

  • Security exposure: Unsupported firmware means known vulnerabilities remain unpatched, leaving your network open to exploits that have been public knowledge for years
  • Compliance risk: PCI DSS, HIPAA, SOC 2, and other regulatory frameworks require supported hardware running current security patches
  • Performance ceiling: Older switches and access points often lack the throughput, port density, or protocol support to handle current traffic volumes
  • Accelerating failure rates: Hardware past its rated lifespan fails at increasing rates, turning every week into a gamble on when the next outage hits

What a Professional Health Check Actually Covers

A comprehensive network health check is not a 20-minute scan with a free diagnostic tool. It involves a physical inspection of cabling, patch panels, and rack infrastructure. It includes active testing of throughput and latency at every critical link in the network path. Wireless site surveys use professional-grade spectrum analyzers, not consumer-grade apps. Firmware and configuration audits compare every network device against vendor-recommended baselines. The deliverable is a detailed report with prioritized recommendations and budgetary cost estimates for each remediation step.

The objective is not to generate a sales pitch for new equipment. It is to give your IT leadership a clear, evidence-based picture of where the infrastructure stands today and what investment it will take to support the organization reliably for the next three to five years.
